## TL;DR

- Six filters decide which supply country wins each ASEAN market: **steering side, EV supply, registration policy, after-sales economics, segment fit, and route logistics.**
- **Chinese supply wins** LHD markets (Cambodia, Laos, Vietnam, Philippines) for SUVs, EVs, and selected pickups.
- **Japanese supply wins** RHD markets (Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia) for almost every used-car segment.
- The two supply chains do not overlap and importers should not try to straddle both in the first 12-24 months.
- This hub links our full coverage of each filter with quick-decision guidance for new importers.

## How to use this framework

Most cross-border used-car decisions get made on price alone, and that is the source of most expensive mistakes. The right decision process is sequential and runs through six filters in order. **You only get to the price comparison after the first five filters narrow your options to a real shortlist.**

Below is the framework. Each step links to the full deep-dive piece, so this page works as the navigation hub for our complete series.

## Filter 1 — Steering side (RHD vs LHD)

This is the binary filter that decides which **half** of ASEAN you can serve from your chosen supply country. Get this wrong and the car cannot be registered, period.

- **LHD markets**: 🇰🇭 Cambodia (since 2022), 🇱🇦 Laos, 🇻🇳 Vietnam, 🇵🇭 Philippines, 🇲🇲 Myanmar (commercial).
- **RHD markets**: 🇹🇭 Thailand, 🇮🇩 Indonesia, 🇲🇾 Malaysia, 🇸🇬 Singapore, 🇧🇳 Brunei, 🇲🇲 Myanmar (private).

China builds and exports LHD only. Japan builds and exports RHD only. **Your destination market locks your supply country.**

→ [RHD vs LHD: The Hidden Constraint That Decides Which Country You Can Sell To](https://blog.ucarsea.com/rhd-vs-lhd-asean-importers-2026/)

## Filter 2 — Country policy windows

Each ASEAN market has its own age caps, duty stacks, and emission standards. The current policy state shifts faster than most international news covers.

**Laos lifted its age cap in 2025** — a fresh window for 2018-2020 vehicles into Vientiane that did not exist in 2024:

**Vietnam holds a strict 5-year age cap** plus high consumption tax on larger displacements. Pair the duty math with the next filter:

## Filter 3 — EV supply asymmetry

If your buyer wants EVs, your supply country is settled before you even start.

**Japan exports more used cars than any country on earth — except when those cars are electric.** Japanese domestic EV penetration sits below 3%, which makes used Japanese EV supply structurally near-zero in 2026. China is the only country shipping used EVs at scale.

→ [China vs Japan: The EV Question — Why Japan Has No Used EV Supply](https://blog.ucarsea.com/china-vs-japan-ev-supply-asean-2026/)

→ [Why Chinese EVs Are Flooding Southeast Asia in 2026](https://blog.ucarsea.com/chinese-evs-flooding-southeast-asia-2026/)

If you cover any LHD market and want EV exposure, China is the supplier. Full stop. There is no Japanese alternative for used EV inventory at the volume an importer needs.

## Filter 4 — Route logistics

The math changes country by country. Two corridors stand out as structurally favored for Chinese supply:

**China → Laos via Kunming-Vientiane railway** delivers in 36 hours overland, vs 25+ days sea-then-overland from Japan. Cost-per-unit is 40-60% lower than the legacy Japanese maritime route:

→ [China to Laos Car Shipping via Kunming: 36 Hours and Three Risks](https://blog.ucarsea.com/china-to-laos-car-shipping-kunming/)

→ [Why Laos Importers Increasingly Look North](https://blog.ucarsea.com/china-vs-japan-laos-importers-look-north/)

**China → Cambodia via Sihanoukville** runs 6-9 days sea freight from Shenzhen. The post-2022 RHD ban removed the Japanese auction option entirely from this corridor:

→ [China vs Japan: Used Car Sourcing for Cambodia in 2026](https://blog.ucarsea.com/china-vs-japan-used-cars-cambodia-2026/)

**The Vietnam corridor** (Shenzhen / Guangzhou → Hai Phong) works but Japanese sea-freight via Yokohama is competitive on cost (though not on EVs). This is where segment selection inside Filter 5 matters most.

## Filter 5 — Segment fit

This is where the most expensive mistakes happen. The map is not "Chinese cars vs Japanese cars" — it is **which segments inside which countries**.

**Where Chinese supply wins clearly:**
- EVs in any LHD market with charging infrastructure (Hanoi, HCMC, Phnom Penh, Vientiane).
- Mid-size SUVs (Haval H6, Geely Boyue, Chery Tiggo 8) at the $20-35K used-import band.
- Light pickups for industrial/regional buyers (GWM Wingle, Foton, JAC).

**Where Japanese supply still wins:**
- Executive sedans (Camry, Accord, Mazda 6) — brand loyalty + resale value premium.
- Family minivans (Innova, Xpander) — dealer network + parts depth.
- Top-tier pickups (Hilux, Ranger) — provincial reliability premium.

The full case-by-case breakdown for Cambodia and Vietnam — the two markets where this segment math matters most — is in the country pieces above.

## Filter 6 — After-sales economics

Year five is the trust deposit. The customer who calls their mechanic in year five and waits two weeks for a control arm is not coming back to you for the next car.

Japanese cars hold a real after-sales moat from 40 years of parts-and-service depth in ASEAN. Chinese brands have closed this gap selectively: BYD, Geely, and Haval have real service networks in capital cities. Tier-3 Chinese brands (Hongqi, NIO, JAC, Foton) do not.

## The decision tree

Run any cross-border purchase decision through this tree in order:

```
1. Destination market?
   ├── Cambodia / Laos / Vietnam / Philippines → LHD → Source from China
   └── Thailand / Indonesia / Malaysia / Singapore → RHD → Source from Japan
   
2. Target customer wants an EV?
   ├── Yes → China only (Japan has no used-EV supply)
   └── No → continue to filter 3
   
3. Destination market policy:
   ├── Age cap acceptable for the vintage you're sourcing?
   ├── Duty stack absorbable in your resale margin?
   └── Emission standard met?
   
4. Segment fit:
   ├── Sedan / Minivan / Premium pickup → Japanese brand likely wins resale
   ├── SUV / EV / Light pickup / Mid-market → Chinese brand competitive or wins
   └── Niche / luxury → Case-by-case
   
5. After-sales:
   ├── Tier 1 or Tier 2 brand support in destination market?
   ├── If Chinese: BYD, Geely, Haval, MG, Chery → OK
   └── If Chinese Tier 3 (Hongqi, JAC, Foton, Xpeng, NIO) → avoid for now
   
6. Cost comparison:
   ├── Landed cost (CIF + duty + clearance)
   ├── Year-5 service cost differential
   └── Expected resale at year 5
   → Net margin to the dealer
```

## Worked example: a Cambodian dealer looking at $20K imports for 2026

Customer: a Phnom Penh dealer who has previously sourced 2017-2019 Toyota Camry from Japan and now needs to pivot post-RHD-ban.

1. **Filter 1**: Cambodia is LHD. **China supplier.**
2. **Filter 2**: Cambodia duty stack ~50-65% on CIF for a $15K FOB sedan. Budget for $22-25K landed.
3. **Filter 3**: Customer base is split between conventional and EV-curious. Decide segment focus.
4. **Filter 4**: Shenzhen → Sihanoukville. 7-9 days. Predictable.
5. **Filter 5 + 6**: Two viable SKUs:
   - **2020 BYD Han EV** — landed cost ~$22K, year-5 service premium vs Camry +$300-500/yr, resale at year 5 ~55-65% of original.
   - **2020 Toyota Camry 2.5G LHD (Chinese-built)** — landed cost ~$23K, year-5 service standard, resale at year 5 ~64-73%.

The decision: BYD if the dealer's buyers value modernity and run cost; Camry if they value resale. Either way, **China is the supplier** — there is no Japanese option here in 2026.

The single worst decision: trying to source the same Camry from Japan, paying $1,800 for a steering-side conversion, and discovering at port that Sihanoukville no longer clears converted RHD vehicles. That mistake still happens monthly in the Cambodia trade.
